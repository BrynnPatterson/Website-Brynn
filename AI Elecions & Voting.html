03 / Analysis

Who AI leaves exposed

AI's impact on elections isn't evenly distributed. The same tools that help a well-funded county flag disinformation in hours can take a small, understaffed office weeks to even notice.

"The tools are neutral. The capacity to defend against them is not."

01 — Detection gap

Resourced vs. under-resourced election offices

Large counties and well-funded state agencies increasingly deploy AI-assisted monitoring to catch deepfakes and coordinated disinformation quickly. Rural counties and small-budget jurisdictions — often the same places already short on poll workers — usually have neither the software nor the staff time to do the same, so false content circulates longer before anyone official responds.

02 — Language access

Non-English speaking voters

Content-moderation and fact-checking systems are trained and tuned overwhelmingly on English-language data. Synthetic disinformation in Spanish, Mandarin, Vietnamese, and other languages spoken by large voter blocs is detected later and corrected less consistently — leaving those communities exposed longer to the same tactics.

03 — Voter roll automation

Algorithmic list maintenance

States increasingly use automated matching tools to flag voters for removal from rolls (duplicate registrations, address changes, deceased voters). These systems have a documented history of disproportionate false-positive rates for voters with common surnames, hyphenated names, or frequent movers — patterns that skew toward specific racial and language communities.

04 — Disability & accessibility

Uneven adoption of assistive AI

AI-driven accessibility tools (screen-reading ballot marking devices, voice interfaces) exist, but adoption is inconsistent across jurisdictions and often tied to local budgets. Voters with disabilities in lower-funded areas frequently rely on older, less capable equipment, while also being a documented target for tailored disinformation about voting procedures.

05 — Legal patchwork

Protection depends on your zip code

As of mid-2026, roughly 30 states have enacted election-deepfake laws — meaning a comparable number have not. A synthetic robocall or fabricated candidate video can be illegal to distribute in one state and unregulated just across the border, with no equivalent federal floor in place.

06 — Targeting precision

Micro-targeted synthetic content

Generative AI lowers the cost of producing large volumes of tailored, hyper-local disinformation — a fabricated "your polling place moved" message customized by precinct, for instance. That precision advantages whoever can afford the tooling, and disadvantages communities least equipped to verify claims quickly through trusted local channels.
